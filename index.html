The Snowflake Thought Experiment

White flakes falling, I stretch out my arm, examine them, discovering they are merely drops of liquid water.

Now, instead I allow them to fall on a sheet of cold metal and observe a dusting of snow crystals, then on a sheet of ice the particles are cast as frozen.

A red hot plate later reveals them as hot spheres of liquid then a roaring fire claims them as puffs of steam. Adapted from H. Levy, The Universe of Science

What is the state of those white things in themself, those fickle entities of which I have variously probed and determined to be in many different states?

Whereas properties of stuff are characteristics presumed to be embodied indissolubly with the object itself, we might better ascribe the term quality for some regularity in behaviour.

Quality flirts with our senses while perennially shifting out of our consciousness. It leaves as quickly as it arrives, leaving only iridescence in its wake. The winking glitter is all we have to investigate its nature. E.H.C

The most obvious regularity in recent behaviour of a flake is its character of collective oneness, though this is ultimately transient. That its unit is illusory and non-immutable is not just a function of our mismeasurement.

Immutable Properties or Behavioural Qualities

An ocean traveler has even more vividly the impression that the ocean is made of waves than that it is made of water. Arthur Eddington

Consider the potential redundancy of the word "property":

  • "Water has the property of being slippery when in solid form" can be more succinctly said as water is slippery upon freezing.
  • "Water has the material property that it enables longitudinal waves to propagate faster than if moving through air." Simply: water enables sound waves to propagate faster than air.
  • "The ocean has both the properties of water and of waves." More directly: the ocean behaves as both water and waves.
  • "Water has the property of being gas-like when heated up and solid-like when cooled." That is: water behaves like a gas when heated and a solid when cooled.

This highlights those aspects of an object's behaviour arising from changing conditions. If you have no transitions of states (and thus no verb with which to characterise behaviour), your purported observation through interaction with the object is null, given your mutual thermal and mechanical equilibrium.

Towards States of Independence

The macroscopic state of a fluid depends on the constraint value of one of its thermodynamic observables. Helium molecules are not strongly attracted to each other, so they must be cooled to 4 K before they will liquify. Liquefying by compression (work done by volume reduction) marks the molecular attraction of gas molecules.

The rate of evaporation of a liquid reflects the extent of the fluctuation of its internal energy: some molecules being less bound will have greater kinetic than their binding potential field energy. A boiling liquid vaporises even faster by deploying a secondary vehicle to eject its essence. Not only is there surface evaporation but there is also evaporation from internal bubbles. The vapour pressure within the bubbles must be sufficient for them to expand against the external pressure of the atmosphere.

Key idea
The boiling point is the temperature at which the vapour pressure equals the atmospheric pressure. It is the internal evaporation of water vapour into bubbles, making them expand and rise to burst at the surface, that defines boiling as distinct from surface evaporation.

Microstates and Boltzmann Entropy

Landau and Lifshitz defined the number of complexions as the number of ways $P$ in which $N = n_1 + n_2$ molecules can be divided into two groups $n_1$ and $n_2$:

$$P = \frac{N!}{n_1!\, n_2!}$$

At equilibrium (over sufficiently long a time) $P$ is maximised when $n_1 = n_2 = N/2$. From this, Boltzmann defines entropy:

$$S = k \ln P$$

as a measure of increasing molecular disorder. Equipartition of energy and kinetic theory determine the average thermal energy carried by each microscopic degree of freedom:

$$\langle E \rangle = \tfrac{1}{2} k T$$

for Boltzmann constant $k$ in units of entropy ($\text{J}\,\text{K}^{-1}$). For an ideal gas of $N$ particles in volume $V$, the complexion number per particle becomes:

$$P^N = \frac{V}{N}\left(\frac{m k T}{2\pi\hbar^2}\right)^{3/2} e^{5/2}$$

The initial conditions are forgotten en route to this devolved most probable state. Any initial lack of symmetry (more particles in the left than right) is always destroyed in time.

Property vs Quality
Only when an unchanging set of results from multiple observations (through time and space) is made, can a catalogue of behavioural characteristics be fixed as a property attributed to an object. If nothing can be fixed and nothing appears to be in of itself, the term quality is more apt.

Canonical Objections to Objectification

To measure is to move from equilibrium: to expand some liquid metal linearly, say. That transfer of heat means that no matter how fine-grained our system's isolation, it is coarse-grained enough to be measurable. If an island ensemble of molecules is tranquil enough to be ascribed a temperature, its boundary must seal itself from the rest of the universe. Our probing to discern that measurement must then be adiabatic, which it never can be.

We indeed, rightly considering objects of sense as mere appearances, confess thereby that they are based upon a thing in itself, though we know not this thing as it is in itself, but only know its appearances, viz., the way in which our senses are affected by this unknown something. Immanuel Kant, Prolegomena, §32

Gibbs' imaginary "ensemble" assumes that thermodynamic experiments, unlike those of social science, do not suffer from a lack of repeatability. In statistical physics, at least on the enormous scale of micro-states, counterfactual scenarios are happily played out simultaneously. Gibbs' ensemble is an imaginary set of such experimental replicants.

A theoretical probability of occurrence (say of a gas molecule found in the right compartment after opening a valve) is framed as an "experimental" probability whose population is a suitably chosen ensemble at a definite time. With an ergodic assumption, that a system is unconstrained to go to any state within an allowed energy band, the equilibrium distribution is independent of the initial microstate.

Here we think of system constraints as some parameter held fixed, such as the charge on a capacitor or the volume of gas in a pump cylinder. Some thermodynamic observables are responses to particular constraints: the pressure is the system's response to a volume constraint, while voltage between capacitor plates is a response to charge on them. Other observables such as the refractive index impose boundary conditions that determine the transmission and reflection form of Maxwell's equations.

We note the process of time averaging across a single system's fluctuation, then averaging across the ensemble, commutes with doing the averaging over an ensemble of systems then time-averaging over that. In equilibrium the (observable) fluctuation distribution and the ensemble distribution are identical.

Localised Islands of Tranquility in Thermostatics

Thermodynamics may be better termed thermostatics. Its elements are the ensemble of system states to which can be assigned macroscopic "state" variables such as temperature and pressure. Such state measures are representative of localised aggregates that within themselves are in equilibrium but between are in flux.

These localised islands of tranquility are individually homogeneous enough to be fairly describable by a coarse-grained observable such as temperature. The temperature arises from the microscopic molecular kinetic energy of constituent "atoms" according to:

$$\tfrac{1}{2}m\overline{v^2} = \tfrac{3}{2}kT$$

and can be inferred from the second, third and fourth moments (skewed right tail) of the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution.

Therein lies a conundrum. The static quality assigned at the particular scale of Observer A's coarse-grained view implies no equilibrium exists between neighbouring islands. If it did exist we would just merge the islands. The islands are then necessarily dynamically transitioning according to the Zeroth Law: in mutual contact, being at different temperatures.

An Observer B, with a less coarse-grained (more refined) view of that same system, will infer more granular temperature variations drawn from sparser sampling distributions. What was previously deemed sets of equilibrium distribution for A could for B be representative sets in flux, dynamically moving towards localised equilibrium.

Is this merely a semantic distortion of a truer fundamental statistical mechanical reality, or is it a convenient but artificial picture? The movement away from the reductionist preoccupation with nouns of stuff to verbs of process within complex dynamical systems would seem a step in the right direction.

Emerging Structure and System Constraints

We note in our various probing methods of the snowflake, its equilibrium state, its structural watery essence, never revealed itself as a state in ultimate isolation. Rather we observe its manifold of qualities or behaviours as it transitions between states.

It was Ilya Prigogine who discovered that far-from-equilibrium systems actually exhibit creative self-organisation: internal structure. Quantum chaos occurs when quantum systems exhibit signature chaotic behaviour that results in organising structure at classical limits when $\hbar \rightarrow 0$. Systems whose equations of state are readily drawn up have constraining features that need not possess this emergent structuring.

As long as we can assign probabilities of its various microstates sufficiently accurately, a thermodynamic state may be designated even as the system is still moving towards equilibrium: a shard of glass may take thousands of years to move to its equilibrium crystalline structure.

Constraints and Degrees of Freedom

Randomisation is an efficient expunger of information: as a system approaches equilibrium all details (except for its energy content) of its starting constrained initial state are lost. At thermodynamic equilibrium the number of degrees of freedom equals the number of constraints plus one (implicitly defined by the equation of state).

Working Against the Tidal Chaos

Field energies are associated with the configuration of fields (densities) stored in space. A thermodynamic system possesses a definite internal energy which is the sum of particle (kinetic, rotational, inertial) and field energies. A black body containing thermal infrared radiation contains merely field energy.

In a dissipative process, which marks the moments of onset of turbulence such as the stirring of a viscous liquid, the work done is converted into energy of disorganised small-scale motions: not lost but neither directly recoverable. Heat is the word we use for energy thought of as randomised and free to move between systems. Changes that occur inside thermally isolated systems are adiabatic processes.

Reversibility and Constraint

Systems exert forces on constraints, so work is done and energy enters the system when a constraint is altered. A gas responds to the volume constraint by exerting a pressure $P$, and if the volume $V$ changes, the work done $dW$ on the gas by the walls of the container is:

$$dW = -P\,dV$$

Whereas dissipative work (such as driving an electric current through a resistor) is irreversible, a slow frictionless change of constraint involving laminar flow of fluid system(s) in contact with a heat reservoir does no net work. Pressure remains at equilibrium and constraints may be returned to their original value. Fast-moving constraints, such as a sudden compression, are both dissipative and irreversible because the impulse applied creates a pressure greater than equilibrium pressure.

The famous laminar flow "un-mixing" demonstration. Dye between rotating cylinders appears to reverse, challenging our intuitions about Eddington's thermodynamic arrow of time.

This experiment in idealised form challenges our notion of Eddington's thermodynamic arrow of time. Penrose, in his Cycles of Time, cites such experiments as a source of pause and deliberation. We are but self-organising structures that have risen from a turbulent universe. Our notion of time is the enabler of behaviour and would point to quality trumping property in our description of all transient structures.

Objects of Object-Oriented Programming

An object for us is a self-contained entity embodied with all its characteristics in of itself. Its usage fixes ownership for a form of behaviour either onto the object or onto the environment or both, making it private and proprietary though we publicly agree to the tacit nature of the object's reality.

Objects of Object-Oriented Programming are classified according to their attributes that render them syntactically sensitive to particular routines or methods.

Attributes (data)

  • speed: float
  • direction: vector
  • gear: int
  • colour: str

Methods (behaviour)

  • brake() → decelerate
  • accelerate() → increase speed
  • change_gear() → shift
  • turn() → alter direction

The object carries with it wherever it goes and whenever it is these qualities, reacting in its individual way to the programming environment in which it is placed. In Python:

class Bicycle:
    """A class is the blueprint template of an object,
    defined by its attributes and methods."""

    def __init__(self, speed=0, gear=1):
        self.speed = speed      # attribute: data
        self.gear = gear

    def accelerate(self, increment):
        """method: the behaviour the object possesses"""
        self.speed += increment

    def brake(self, decrement):
        self.speed = max(0, self.speed - decrement)

# The object is an instance of its class
my_bike = Bicycle(speed=12, gear=3)
my_bike.accelerate(5)
print(my_bike.speed)  # 17
The Analogy

Just as thermodynamic "properties" are really behaviours in response to constraints and probing, OOP "attributes" are only meaningful when methods act upon them. The object has no essence independent of interaction.
