The Snowflake Thought Experiment

White flakes falling, I stretch out my arm, examine them, discovering they are merely drops of liquid water.

Now, instead I allow them to fall on a sheet of cold metal and observe a dusting of snow crystals, then on a sheet of ice the particles are cast as frozen.

A red hot plate later reveals them as hot spheres of liquid then a roaring fire claims them as puffs of steam. Adapted from H. Levy, The Universe of Science

What is the state of those white things in themselves, those fickle entities of which I have variously probed and determined to be in many different states?

The question seems innocent enough. Is water really a solid, a liquid, or a gas? Yet the answer appears to depend on the conditions under which it is examined. The same stuff is made to answer differently when temperature, pressure, or surface conditions are changed.

That is the beginning of the problem. Perhaps what we call a thing is not first revealed by what it is, but by how it responds.

Are properties fixed, or do things just behave differently?

An ocean traveler has even more vividly the impression that the ocean is made of waves than that it is made of water. Arthur Eddington

We often speak as though an object carries its properties around like labels stitched into its fabric. But many descriptions that sound like properties may be better understood as regular patterns of behaviour under changing conditions.

  • Instead of saying water has the property of becoming slippery in frozen form, we might say: water becomes slippery when frozen.
  • Instead of saying water has the property of transmitting sound better than air, we might say: water allows sound to travel faster than air.
  • Instead of saying water has the property of being gas-like when heated and solid-like when cooled, we might say: water behaves like a gas when heated and like a solid when cooled.

This is not merely a change of wording. It shifts the focus from static labels to responses. Behaviour enters wherever conditions can vary.

Quality flirts with our senses while perennially shifting out of our consciousness. It leaves as quickly as it arrives, leaving only iridescence in its wake. The winking glitter is all we have to investigate its nature. E.H.C

If a thing is perfectly at equilibrium with us, and never changes under any probing, then there is very little to say. Observation begins where interaction begins, and interaction means change.

Same stuff, different questions

Before introducing technical language, it helps to pause over something simple. A substance does not present all of itself at once. It presents one face when cooled, another when compressed, another when stirred, and another when heated.

So perhaps what we call a property is sometimes not a permanent label attached to a thing, but a regular answer to a regular question. Ask water what it does under cold conditions and it may answer with rigidity. Ask the same water what it does under heat and it answers with flow, then vapour.

The thing has not vanished; what has changed is the circumstance under which it is interrogated.

Key shift
Many so-called properties may be better understood as behaviours under conditions.

What do physicists mean by a state?

In everyday speech, a state sounds like a label: solid, liquid, gas. In physics, it usually means something slightly more precise: a summary description of a system under particular constraints.

The macroscopic state of a fluid depends on quantities such as temperature, pressure, and volume. These are not microscopic snapshots of every molecule. They are large-scale summaries that let us speak meaningfully about what the system is doing as a whole.

Helium molecules, for example, are only weakly attracted to one another, so helium must be cooled to about 4 K before it liquefies. Compression can also push gases toward liquefaction. In both cases, what matters is not a hidden mystical essence but the balance of constraints under which the molecules move.

Already, then, a state is not a thing in isolation. It is a description of a system as it stands under a given set of conditions.

Boiling, evaporation, and why bubbles matter

The rate of evaporation of a liquid reflects fluctuations in its internal energy. Some molecules, being less tightly bound than others, have enough kinetic energy to escape from the surface. That is ordinary evaporation.

Boiling is something more dramatic. It is not just surface escape. It also involves the formation of vapour bubbles inside the liquid. Those bubbles expand only when the vapour pressure within them is sufficient to overcome the surrounding external pressure.

Key idea
The boiling point is the temperature at which the vapour pressure equals the atmospheric pressure. Boiling differs from simple evaporation because vapour forms not only at the surface but throughout the bulk of the liquid.

From what we see to what might be happening underneath

So far we have spoken at the level of what can be seen: ice, water, steam, boiling, flow. Physics often asks a second question: what invisible arrangement could give rise to these visible behaviours?

A glass of water may look calm, yet at the microscopic level its molecules are jostling, colliding and exchanging energy. The visible state is therefore a summary of many unseen possibilities.

This is where a useful distinction enters:

  • Macrostate: the broad description we observe, such as temperature, pressure, or phase.
  • Microstate: one particular detailed arrangement of all the particles.

Once that distinction is made, entropy ceases to be a mysterious word and begins to look like a way of counting possibilities.

Microstates and Boltzmann entropy

Landau and Lifshitz defined the number of complexions as the number of ways $P$ in which $N = n_1 + n_2$ molecules can be divided into two groups $n_1$ and $n_2$:

$$P = \frac{N!}{n_1!\, n_2!}$$

At equilibrium, over sufficiently long a time, $P$ is maximised when $n_1 = n_2 = N/2$. Boltzmann then defines entropy by

$$S = k \ln P$$

Entropy here is not a misty metaphysical fog. It is a measure of how many microscopic arrangements are compatible with the macroscopic state we observe.

Equipartition of energy and kinetic theory further tell us that the average thermal energy carried by each microscopic degree of freedom is

$$\langle E \rangle = \tfrac{1}{2} k T$$

The broad lesson is clear: the system drifts toward macrostates that can be realised in the largest number of ways. Initial asymmetry is not impossible, merely fragile.

Property versus quality, revisited
Only when an unchanging set of results is produced again and again do we begin to catalogue stable behaviours as “properties”. Where nothing remains fixed except a pattern of response, the word quality may be the better fit.
Advanced note

For stronger readers, the full statistical-mechanical machinery goes much further than the simple counting argument above. But for the philosophical line of this essay, the key point is already present: a macrostate is a coarse summary over many microstates.

Why equilibrium can be local rather than perfect

Thermodynamics might often be better called thermostatics. It gives us a language for describing systems that are stable enough, at some scale, to be assigned state variables such as temperature and pressure.

But such stability need not extend everywhere at once. It may be local. A system may contain little regions that are sufficiently uniform to be treated as temporary islands of equilibrium even while the larger whole remains in flux.

The temperature associated with one such localised region arises from the microscopic kinetic energy of its constituents:

$$\tfrac{1}{2}m\overline{v^2} = \tfrac{3}{2}kT$$

For a gas in equilibrium, the distribution of molecular speeds is described by the Maxwell–Boltzmann speed distribution. If \(v\) is the speed of a molecule of mass \(m\), then the probability density is

$$ f(v)=4\pi \left(\frac{m}{2\pi kT}\right)^{3/2} v^2 e^{-mv^2/(2kT)} $$

This curve explains why higher temperatures broaden the distribution and shift more molecules into the high-speed tail. The macroscopic temperature is therefore not attached to one molecule, but to the statistical spread of speeds across very many of them.

That temperature is a coarse-grained summary. It is real and useful, but it does not abolish the microscopic motion underneath it.

What one observer treats as a stable thermal region, another observer with finer resolution may treat as a set of smaller regions still moving toward equilibrium. Stability, then, is scale-dependent.

This does not make the language of thermodynamics false. It makes it coarse-grained, and therefore powerful.

Emerging structure and system constraints

Our various ways of probing the snowflake never uncover a final isolated essence. What we meet instead are states, transitions, and constraints. Yet this does not mean that the world is featureless. Quite the contrary: structure can emerge precisely because systems are constrained and driven.

It was Ilya Prigogine who emphasised that far-from-equilibrium systems can generate organised behaviour rather than merely descend into bland uniformity. Under suitable conditions, flows, gradients and exchanges with the surroundings permit self-organisation.

Some systems are simple enough that their equations of state can be written down cleanly. Others develop vortices, patterns, instabilities and long-lived structures. A shard of glass, for example, may take thousands of years to approach its fully crystalline equilibrium. It is not frozen in perfection; it is trapped in an extraordinarily slow journey.

Constraints and freedom
Randomisation is an efficient destroyer of detailed information. As a system approaches equilibrium, most traces of its constrained initial state are washed out. Yet away from equilibrium, constraints can also generate structure by forcing matter and energy into organised patterns.

Work, reversibility, and the pull toward disorder

Field energies are associated with the configuration of fields stored in space. A thermodynamic system possesses a definite internal energy which includes particle energies and field energies alike. A black body filled with thermal radiation contains field energy even without lumps of matter sloshing about inside it.

In a dissipative process, such as stirring a viscous liquid, work is converted into disorganised small-scale motion. The energy is not lost, but it becomes unrecoverable in any neat mechanical sense. Heat is the name we give to energy when it is randomised and available to flow between systems.

Reversibility and constraint

Systems exert forces on constraints, so work is done when a constraint is altered. If a gas responds to the volume constraint by exerting a pressure $P$, then for a volume change $dV$ the work done on the gas is

$$dW = -P\,dV$$

Slow, frictionless changes can in principle be reversed. Sudden compressions cannot. Once turbulence, friction or resistive heating enters, the system remembers the violence of the intervention not as useful organised motion but as dispersed microscopic agitation.

The laminar flow “un-mixing” demonstration. The reversal seems to challenge our intuitions about the arrow of time.

Such demonstrations force us to think carefully. Disorder is not merely visual mess. It concerns recoverability, reversibility and the number of microscopic ways a system may continue.

Why measuring a system is never completely innocent

To measure is already to disturb. A thermometer works because heat flows. A pressure gauge works because a force is transmitted. Measurement is never the extraction of a pure property from a sealed essence. It is an interaction that moves a system, however slightly, away from isolation.

We indeed, rightly considering objects of sense as mere appearances, confess thereby that they are based upon a thing in itself, though we know not this thing as it is in itself, but only know its appearances, viz., the way in which our senses are affected by this unknown something. Immanuel Kant, Prolegomena, §32

If an aggregate is tranquil enough to be assigned a temperature, then it must be sufficiently insulated from its surroundings to sustain that local identity. Yet our attempt to measure it necessarily breaches that tranquility. The coarser the system boundary, the easier the measurement; the finer the boundary, the less the system tolerates inspection.

This is not an embarrassment for science. It is a reminder that observables belong to relationships between system, apparatus and environment, not to a pure thing viewed from nowhere.

Why physicists imagine many copies of one system

Once we begin counting microstates, a practical problem appears. No real experiment can watch one system explore every allowed configuration over arbitrarily long times. Statistical physics therefore introduces an idealisation: the ensemble.

An ensemble is an imaginary collection of many copies of the same system, each in a different allowed microstate. The copies do not need to exist physically in the laboratory. Their purpose is conceptual. They allow us to calculate stable averages all at once.

What one real system might explore in time, the ensemble displays side by side in thought.

With the ergodic assumption, the time-average of one sufficiently unconstrained system is treated as equivalent to the ensemble average over many imagined copies. In equilibrium, the observable fluctuation distribution and the ensemble distribution coincide.

Here again the philosophical point returns: what we call an object is often something held together by the stability of a description, not by direct access to all of its microscopic detail.

A programming analogy: what is an object?

Object-oriented programming offers a suggestive analogy. In programming, an object is often treated as a self-contained entity endowed with attributes and methods. But even here, its meaning does not come from a hidden essence. It comes from what can be done with it and how it responds within an environment.

Attributes (data)

  • speed: float
  • direction: vector
  • gear: int
  • colour: str

Methods (behaviour)

  • brake() → decelerate
  • accelerate() → increase speed
  • change_gear() → shift
  • turn() → alter direction

In Python, for example:

class Bicycle:
    """A class is the blueprint template of an object,
    defined by its attributes and methods."""

    def __init__(self, speed=0, gear=1):
        self.speed = speed
        self.gear = gear

    def accelerate(self, increment):
        self.speed += increment

    def brake(self, decrement):
        self.speed = max(0, self.speed - decrement)

my_bike = Bicycle(speed=12, gear=3)
my_bike.accelerate(5)
print(my_bike.speed)  # 17
The analogy
The object becomes intelligible through interaction. Its attributes matter because methods act upon them. In a similar way, thermodynamic “properties” often become meaningful only through the responses a system gives when it is constrained, measured or perturbed.

Conclusion: from things to patterns of response

The temptation is always to think that science reveals the hidden noun behind appearances: the complete thing in itself. But the path through thermodynamics suggests something subtler.

What becomes available to us first is not essence but response: how a system behaves under changed conditions, how structure appears under constraint, how order dissolves into probability, and how stable descriptions arise from restless underlying motion.

In that sense, science often begins not with substances but with transformations. The snowflake, the liquid, the vapour and the measured temperature are not rival realities. They are different windows onto a system whose identity is inseparable from the conditions under which it is probed.

Perhaps, then, quality sometimes outruns property. What we meet first is not the object stripped bare, but the pattern of its possible behaviours.
